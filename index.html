🧬
Closest Relatives

Gorillas share 98.3% of their DNA with humans, making them our second-closest living relatives after chimpanzees. The four gorilla subspecies — western lowland, Cross River, eastern lowland, and mountain — are all endangered or critically endangered.

💪
Incredible Strength

Adult male gorillas (silverbacks) can lift over 1,800 lbs — roughly 10 times the strength of a human. Despite this, gorillas are predominantly gentle and non-aggressive, preferring bluffs and chest-beating to actual combat.

🌿
Plant-Based Giants

Gorillas are almost entirely herbivorous, consuming up to 40 lbs of vegetation per day — including leaves, stems, fruit, and bark. They occasionally supplement with insects like termites and ants to get extra protein.

🗣️
Sign Language & Communication

Gorillas can learn hundreds of signs in American Sign Language. Koko the gorilla famously mastered over 1,000 signs and understood ~2,000 spoken words, demonstrating remarkable cognitive depth and emotional intelligence.

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦
Social Structure

Gorillas live in family groups called troops, typically led by a dominant silverback. Troops average 5–10 members but can exceed 30. The silverback makes decisions on movement, foraging, and defense of the group.

🛌
Nest Builders

Gorillas build new sleeping nests every night from branches and leaves, either on the ground or in trees. They spend roughly 12 hours a day resting, and researchers use nest counts to estimate gorilla population sizes.

🏥
Self-Medicating

Gorillas practice zoopharmacognosy — the ability to select and consume specific plants for medicinal purposes. They've been observed eating plants with antibacterial and antiparasitic properties that they otherwise avoid in normal foraging.

👣
Unique Fingerprints

Like humans, every gorilla has a completely unique set of fingerprints and noseprints. Researchers use noseprint identification (nostrils, ridges, and wrinkles of the nose) as a reliable method to individually identify wild gorillas.

🌍
Critical to Ecosystems

Gorillas are a keystone species. By spreading seeds through their dung across vast forest territories, they help maintain the biodiversity of Central African rainforests. Their decline would have cascading effects on forest regeneration.

🦍 In Memoriam

The Legend of Harambe

Harambe was a western lowland gorilla born on May 27, 1999, at the Gladys Porter Zoo in Brownsville, Texas. He was transferred to the Cincinnati Zoo and Botanical Garden in 2014, where he lived until his tragic death on May 28, 2016.

When a three-year-old boy fell into the gorilla enclosure, zoo officials made the heartbreaking decision to shoot Harambe to protect the child. The event ignited a global conversation about wildlife conservation, captive animal welfare, and our relationship with the natural world.

Harambe was not just a gorilla — he became a symbol. His passing prompted millions to learn about the plight of western lowland gorillas, listed as Critically Endangered by the IUCN, with fewer than 100,000 individuals remaining in the wild.

🔬 Deep Knowledge

More Gorilla Science

🧠

Remarkable Intelligence

Gorillas demonstrate abstract thinking, use rudimentary tools (sticks to test water depth, branches as ladders), exhibit empathy, and have been shown to understand cause-and-effect relationships. Their problem-solving ability rivals that of young children.

👶

Long Childhood

Gorilla infants nurse for up to 3 years and stay close to their mothers for 4–5 years. This extended childhood allows them to learn complex social behaviors, foraging techniques, and tool use — a hallmark of highly intelligent species.

❤️

Emotions & Grief

Gorillas display clear emotional responses including joy, sadness, and grief. They have been documented mourning deceased group members, staying with bodies for extended periods. They form deep, lasting bonds that can persist for decades.

🔊

The Chest Beat

Gorillas beat their chests with cupped hands — producing a sound audible nearly a mile away — to communicate information about their size, health, and location. Contrary to popular belief, it is usually a bluff or social signal, not a sign of aggression.

🌡️

Climate Sensitivity

Mountain gorillas (Gorilla beringei beringei) live at altitudes up to 14,700 ft and have developed thicker fur to survive cold temperatures. Climate change is altering their bamboo-rich habitats, threatening food availability year-round.

🛡️

Conservation Progress

Mountain gorillas are the only great ape whose population is actually increasing — rising from ~620 in 2008 to over 1,000 today — thanks to sustained anti-poaching efforts, veterinary care, and eco-tourism revenue in Uganda, Rwanda, and DRC.

🌐 Who We Are

About AlfaHarambe

Our Mission

AlfaHarambe is an independent fan site dedicated to the memory of Harambe and the broader conservation of western lowland gorillas. We believe that education, empathy, and awareness are the most powerful tools for wildlife protection.

This site was built with love, code, and a deep respect for one of nature's most magnificent animals. We strive to separate fact from internet folklore and present accurate, thoughtful information about gorilla biology, behavior, and conservation.

Harambe's Legacy

Harambe's story transcended the internet. While the memes brought laughs, the underlying conversation raised genuine questions about zoo welfare, endangered species breeding programs, and what we owe to the animals we keep in captivity. His legacy continues to fuel donations to organizations like the Dian Fossey Gorilla Fund and the African Wildlife Foundation.
